The purpose of this post is to gather together as many sensible, defensible reasons against the use of (or reliance on) Generative AI as I can; for myself, really. To weed out what it is exactly that bothers me the most. The world hardly needs another AI opinion piece (destined to be gobbled up by AI itself), but in compiling these points I thought I'd share them in case other like-minded folk found them useful.

It is not a rant against every possible use of AI or a condemnation of those who use it. I myself use Mistral the odd time for help with very specific web searches (partly due to the <span id="search-results-cite">general degradation of search engine results</span>) <a href="#search-results-src"><sup>1</sup></a> , but if you don't (or rarely) use AI and feel like you're being "left behind", perhaps this piece will provide some solace.

For each issue raised, common objections are countered, and a visual "solvability" scale is provided to rate how feasible a fix is for the issue in question.


## Colossal Cultural Thievery

It all begins with data gathering; the foundation upon which LLMs (Large Language Models) and other GPTs (Generative Pre-trained Transformers) are built (hereafter I'll refer to both of these as Gen AI). And this first stage is rife with ethical concerns.

If an individual appropriates the artistic or intellectual work of another without permission or attribution, it is a clear case of theft in violation of copyright law. If a corporation does it however, and does so on a global scale, indiscriminately hoovering up all of humanity's hard-earned cultural treasures in the process, it is exempted by governments and celebrated by proponents as "fair use", even when that use is at its core primed to put the livelihoods of those it has stolen the most from at risk. Writers, artists, musicians and others who have dedicated themselves to their craft are pilfered from without a penny's compensation.

It is difficult not to see this as theft, pure and simple.

*Objection: is this not any different from how humans learn from the best works of art and literature available to them?*

It's more accurate to say that a Gen AI system will sample or lift rather than learn from its training material. This material is first consumed (corresponding to the pre-training phase of a GPT) then translated into statistical probabilities (corresponding to the transforming phase) from which it can later produce output (corresponding to the generative phase). A Gen AI system does not possess any inherent ability to understand or appreciate this material, which is a critical component of learning. 

*Objection: If machine learning is not true learning, how does a Gen AI system produce novel output?*

While a Gen AI system doesn't retrieve content from a database in any conventional sense, its source data is nonetheless retained, albeit in a highly obfuscated form. This explains why it is still possible for it to output, verbatim, copyrighted passages of text - and as recent studies have shown, entire books - that it has been trained on. **(cn)** Turning down the "temperature" setting of a Gen AI system aligns it more closely with its source material, while turning it up creates more random departures. The latter is what can lead to novelty, but also hallucinations (and at the highest settings, pure nonsense).

*Objection: So what if the data is stolen? Have you never made a mix-tape or torrented a movie?*

There's a big difference between illicitly sharing copyrighted content and consuming it in order to automate the creation of derivative (and often competing) works while neither crediting nor compensating its original creators. If I ever made a mix-tape, I at least didn't pass the music off as my own.

Solvability: 6/10. While this an easy fix in theory (i.e. be ethical from the outset and only collect data in the public domain), AI companies have already gotten away with illegally scraping more copyrighted material than you could possibly imagine, so it's difficult to see how to turn back the clock on this one. With some political will it's possible, but unlikely.

## The Environment

Thanks to Gen AI, we're now sprinting rather than running towards climate disaster. 

If you had to choose a single reason to oppose the widespread use of Gen AI, this one is a strong candidate. While our efforts to stop runaway climate change have been like a slow crawl across a hot desert floor, Gen AI has been the boot that came along to kick sand in our eyes.

GPT 

Generative Pre-trained Transformers (GPTs), or in their most popular incarnation, AI bots, are everywhere. In 2024, bots were responsible for over half of global internet traffic, giving further credence to the 'dead internet' theory. But is the internet the only thing AI bots are killing (or deadening at least)?

The messaging "Learn AI or get left behind" is equally ubiquitous, whether it takes the form of a LinkedIn post by a self-styled thought leader, or a new icon in the software you use every day that hops and wiggles, clamouring to be clicked. "I'm a new AI-powered feature that will 10x your productivity!" it promises.

But what really gets left behind when we use AI? And are there sensible, defensible reasons to opt out of its use (or not opt-in in the first place)?

* Product over Process: AI is all about the final product, and the (much claimed) efficiency gains that come with that are at the expense of the process. Wow, ChatGPT whipped up a first draft in seconds - what a way to supercharge my productivity! Perhaps; perhaps not. Could there be value in staring at the blank page, or the blinking cursor? Could you in fact learn more during the struggle to fill out that first line? 


Are you a thought leader or thought enfeebler?

Here are several recent studies and reports that link the use of generative AI with cognitive decline or related concerns:

**MIT Study (2025)**: A study from MIT found that participants who exclusively used AI to help write essays showed weaker brain connectivity, lower memory retention, and a fading sense of ownership over their work. These cognitive declines persisted even after participants stopped using the AI, suggesting long-term effects on brain activity. https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.08872

**University of Pennsylvania (2024–2025)**: Researchers discovered that students who relied on AI for practice problems performed worse on tests compared to those who completed assignments without AI assistance. The study warns that over-reliance on AI may undermine the development of problem-solving and analytical skills. https://www.forbes.com/sites/chriswestfall/2024/12/18/the-dark-side-of-ai-tracking-the-decline-of-human-cognitive-skills/

**Microsoft & Carnegie Mellon University (2025)**: A survey of 319 knowledge workers found that over-reliance on generative AI may erode critical thinking skills. The more users trusted AI-generated outputs, the less cognitive effort they applied to tasks. https://businessesgrow.com/2025/02/12/cognitive-decline/

**Polytechnique Insights (2025)**: A review of multiple studies highlights the risk of "cognitive atrophy" due to generative AI use, citing reduced memory retention and cognitive engagement, especially in tasks like essay writing. https://www.polytechnique-insights.com/en/columns/neuroscience/generative-ai-the-risk-of-cognitive-atrophy/

These studies collectively indicate that while generative AI can enhance productivity, its overuse may contribute to cognitive decline, particularly in areas like memory, critical thinking, and problem-solving.
